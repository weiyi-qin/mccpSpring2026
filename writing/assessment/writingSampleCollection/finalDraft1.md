# Constrained Online Convex Optimization: Keeping Pace with Dynamics of Both Loss and Constraint

## INTRODUCTION

**[Move 1 – Establishing a Territory]** Online Convex Optimization (OCO) has emerged as a fundamental framework at the intersection of machine learning, operations research, and game theory, playing a pivotal role in modeling sequential decision-making problems under uncertainty (Hazan, 2016; Shalev-Shwartz, 2012). In real-world applications, decision-makers are frequently required to make a sequence of choices under incomplete information and continuously evolving environments, with the overarching goal of minimizing cumulative losses over time. As application scenarios have grown increasingly complex, the standard OCO framework has naturally evolved into Constrained Online Convex Optimization (COCO). COCO imposes a more rigorous structure by demanding that the optimization of the objective function must be accompanied by the satisfaction of specific resource, physical, or safety constraints at every time step. This advanced framework holds immense practical value in critical systems such as advertisement placement (Balseiro, Lu, & Mirrokni, 2020), load balancing (Hsu et al., 2021), network virtualization (Shi, Lin, & Fahmy, 2021), and resource allocation (Wang et al., 2023). In these complex systems, environmental dynamism—such as fluctuating electricity demands or shifting market prices—is the norm rather than the exception. Consequently, it is imperative that optimization algorithms possess the agility to acutely capture and adapt to the dynamic variations inherent in both the loss functions and the constraint conditions. While early research predominantly focused on analyzing static regret bounds in stationary environments (Zinkevich, 2003), recent scholarly attention has decisively shifted towards the more formidable challenge of dynamic environments, seeking to strike a delicate balance between minimizing dynamic regret and strictly controlling constraint violations (Eshraghi & Liang, 2020; Hall & Willett, 2015).

**[Move 2 – Identifying a Niche]** Despite the significant theoretical and practical advancements achieved by existing COCO algorithms, the current state of research exhibits several critical limitations that hinder their deployment in highly dynamic, real-world systems. First and foremost, a substantial majority of state-of-the-art algorithms designed to handle time-varying constraints rely heavily on the Slater condition (Chen, Ling, & Giannakis, 2017; Yu, Neely, & Wei, 2017). The Slater condition assumes the existence of a strictly feasible interior point that satisfies all constraints with a margin. However, in highly dynamic and stringent real-world systems, constraints can fluctuate wildly, making the Slater condition excessively restrictive and often impossible to satisfy, thereby severely limiting the applicability of these existing algorithms. Secondly, when addressing constraint violations, the prevailing literature predominantly adopts the metric of "soft constraint violation" or "long-term constraints" (Mahdavi, Jin, & Yang, 2012; Jenatton, Huang, & Archambeau, 2016). This paradigm permits constraint violations at certain time steps to be compensated by strict compliance at others. While mathematically convenient, this approach is fundamentally flawed for safety-critical applications. In domains such as autonomous driving or power grid stabilization, any instantaneous constraint violation can precipitate catastrophic system failures. Therefore, evaluating "hard constraint violations," which strictly prohibit any form of compensatory violation over time, is of paramount necessity (Yuan & Lamperski, 2018). Currently, achieving optimal dynamic regret while strictly controlling hard constraint violations without relying on the Slater condition remains a profound and unresolved gap in the literature, particularly in environments characterized by the dual dynamics of both loss and constraint functions (Guo et al., 2022; Sinha & Vaze, 2024).

**[Move 3 – Occupying the Niche]** To bridge the aforementioned research gaps, this study aims to propose a novel online learning algorithm, termed Constrained Online Learning with Doubly-bounded Queue (COLDQ). The central objective of this research is to design an optimization mechanism capable of strictly controlling hard constraint violations and minimizing dynamic regret in dynamic environments, entirely independent of the restrictive Slater condition. Specifically, this research introduces an innovative "doubly-bounded virtual queue" technique. By simultaneously establishing both lower and upper bounds for the virtual queue, the proposed algorithm enables tight, instantaneous control over constraint violations. Through a novel Lyapunov drift analysis, this study intends to demonstrate that the COLDQ algorithm can adaptively achieve optimal dynamic regret and hard constraint violation bounds that smoothly approach the best-known theoretical limits as the dynamics of the losses and constraints diminish. Furthermore, this research will introduce an expert-tracking variation of COLDQ, providing a robust solution that achieves identical performance bounds without requiring any prior knowledge of the system's dynamics.

## LITERATURE REVIEW

**[Move 1 – Thematic Overview]** This section aims to review and synthesize the relevant literature in the field of Constrained Online Convex Optimization (COCO), with a specific focus on the theoretical evolution and inherent limitations of algorithms designed to handle time-varying constraints and dynamic environments. To clearly define the scope of this review, the analysis will be anchored around two core performance metrics: dynamic regret (the cumulative difference in loss between the algorithm's decisions and the optimal dynamic sequence of decisions) and hard constraint violation (the cumulative non-negative magnitude of constraint breaches). The existing literature is categorized into three primary themes: standard online convex optimization and static regret, online learning with soft/long-term constraints, and recent advances targeting dynamic regret and hard constraints. Through a critical examination of these themes, this review seeks to expose the theoretical bottlenecks encountered by existing methods when confronting dual dynamics, thereby establishing a robust theoretical foundation for the proposed COLDQ algorithm.

**[Move 2 – Critical Analysis]**
**Theme 1: Standard Online Convex Optimization and Static Regret**
Early research in OCO primarily concentrated on unconstrained optimization problems or those with fixed, time-invariant feasible domains. The seminal work by Zinkevich (2003) introduced the Online Gradient Descent (OGD) algorithm, establishing a static regret bound of $O(T)$ under general convex assumptions. Subsequent research further optimized this framework, providing logarithmic regret bounds for strongly convex functions (Hazan, Agarwal, & Kale, 2007). However, a shared and fundamental limitation of these classical algorithms lies in their evaluation benchmark: static regret. Static regret compares the algorithm's performance against a single, fixed optimal decision determined in hindsight. In highly dynamic environments, the true optimal decision fluctuates significantly over time. Consequently, static regret fails to accurately reflect an algorithm's adaptability, prompting researchers to pivot towards the more rigorous metric of dynamic regret (Jadbabaie et al., 2015; Mokhtari et al., 2016; Zhang et al., 2017).

**Theme 2: Online Learning with Soft/Long-term Constraints**
To accommodate constraints that evolve over time and to reduce the computational complexity of projection operations, Mahdavi, Jin, and Yang (2012) pioneered the concept of long-term constraints. This paradigm permits algorithms to violate constraints at specific time steps, provided that the cumulative violation over the entire time horizon grows sublinearly. Within this framework, virtual queue techniques based on Lyapunov optimization have been extensively adopted to bound soft constraint violations (Yu & Neely, 2020). For time-varying constraints, several studies have proposed saddle-point-type or virtual-queue-based algorithms to achieve sublinear static regret and soft constraint violations (Cao, Zhang, & Poor, 2021; Wei, Yu, & Neely, 2020). Furthermore, dynamic regret under soft constraints has also been explored (Cao & Liu, 2019; Liu et al., 2022). Nevertheless, the fatal vulnerability of this school of thought is its tolerance for "compensatory violations." Traditional virtual queue methods can only guarantee the stability of the queue length in the long run, implying that the algorithm might incur massive, unacceptable hard constraint violations in the short term, rendering it inadequate for safety-critical scenarios.

**Theme 3: Recent Advances in Dynamic Regret and Hard Constraints**
In recent years, the academic community has begun to tackle more stringent performance metrics, attempting to simultaneously bound dynamic regret and hard constraint violations. Yuan and Lamperski (2018) introduced the stronger notion of hard constraint violation that does not allow any compensated violation. For fixed constraints, Guo et al. (2022) provided the best-known $O(1)$ hard constraint violation while maintaining $O(T)$ static regret. For time-varying constraints, recent studies have made significant strides. Yi et al. (2021) and Yi et al. (2023) analyzed regret and cumulative constraint violations under distributed settings. More recently, Sinha and Vaze (2024) achieved the current best $O(T \log T)$ hard constraint violation and $O(T)$ static regret for adversarial constraints. Although these studies have successfully achieved sublinear dynamic regret bounds (Zhao & Zhang, 2021; Zhang, Lu, & Zhou, 2018), their mechanisms for controlling hard constraint violations almost universally depend on the Slater condition or fail to recover the optimal $O(1)$ violation when the system dynamics diminish.

**[Move 3 – Research Gaps]** A comprehensive synthesis of the aforementioned literature reveals a glaring theoretical disconnect within the COCO domain. Specifically, as the dynamics of the loss and constraint functions decrease, none of the existing hard constraint violation bounds for time-varying constraints smoothly approaches the best-known $O(1)$ violation observed in fixed constraint scenarios (Guo et al., 2022; Sinha & Vaze, 2024). Furthermore, the existing body of work fails to provide an effective mechanism capable of strictly controlling hard constraint violations while simultaneously achieving optimal dynamic regret bounds without relying on the Slater condition. Traditional, singly-bounded virtual queues are fundamentally ill-equipped to handle the severe dual dynamics of simultaneously changing losses and constraints.

**[Move 4 – Conclusion of the Literature Review]** In conclusion, traditional static regret analyses and soft constraint models are no longer sufficient to meet the rigorous demands of modern, complex dynamic systems. Concurrently, existing dynamic COCO algorithms remain severely handicapped by their reliance on the stringent Slater condition and their inability to keep pace with diminishing system dynamics. This landscape underscores an urgent necessity to develop novel constraint-handling mechanisms. To overcome this theoretical bottleneck, a fundamental redesign of the traditional virtual queue structure is required. By introducing a "doubly-bounded queue" that restricts both the lower and upper bounds of the queue, this study expects to theoretically eliminate the dependence on the Slater condition. In doing so, it aims to provide unprecedented, smooth, and tight theoretical guarantees for both dynamic regret and hard constraint violations in environments characterized by dual dynamics.
